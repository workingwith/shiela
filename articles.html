The Upskilling Imperative: Why the Best Workers Should Run Toward AI, Not Away From It

S
Shiela Cortez
Strategic Executive Partner · AI-Augmented Ops

AI-Augmented Operations
AI-Augmented Operations Future of Work Upskilling Executive Support

There is a version of the AI conversation that keeps getting repeated, and it goes something like this: AI is coming for your job. Automate or be automated. The robots are winning.

That version isn't entirely wrong. But it's missing the most important part of the story, and I think that gap is costing a lot of talented, capable people their competitive edge.

Here's what I've actually seen working inside operations, executive support, and fast-paced business environments: AI doesn't replace skilled workers. It exposes the gap between workers who are willing to evolve and those who are not.

"The threat isn't artificial intelligence. The threat is staying exactly as you are while everyone around you compounds their capability."

The Real Shift Nobody Is Talking About

The loudest part of the AI conversation is about replacement: which roles are at risk, which tasks will be automated, which industries will be disrupted. And yes, some of that is real. Repetitive, low-judgment work is increasingly being handled by AI tools, and that trend is not slowing down.

But there's a quieter, more consequential shift happening underneath that noise: the workers who are learning to use AI as a force multiplier are becoming dramatically more valuable. Not because they're doing different work, but because they're doing the same work at a level of quality, speed, and depth that simply wasn't possible before.

A skilled executive assistant who uses AI to draft communications, synthesize information, build systems, and generate first cuts of documents is not doing less. She is doing more, with more precision, more consistency, and more capacity. The judgment, the relationships, the understanding of nuance? That's still hers. AI handles the heavy lifting so she can focus on the parts that actually require a human brain.

What "AI-Augmented" Actually Means

I use the phrase "AI-augmented" constantly, and I want to be precise about what it means, because it's not about replacing human expertise with a chatbot.

AI augmentation means layering AI capabilities into your existing work to remove friction, accelerate output, and free up your cognitive bandwidth for the decisions and relationships that only a human can handle.

In practice, for operations and executive support work, that looks like:

  • Using AI to draft the first version of a proposal, then applying your expertise to refine it, personalize it, and make it actually persuasive
  • Building AI-assisted systems that surface the right information at the right time, so you're never digging through inboxes or chasing updates
  • Automating the repeatable parts of a workflow (status reports, meeting summaries, follow-up tracking) so your energy stays on the judgment calls that move things forward
  • Running faster research, sharper analysis, and more comprehensive documentation than would be possible in the same timeframe manually

None of that removes the human. It amplifies the human. The strategic thinking, the relationship management, the contextual judgment: none of that gets automated away. What does go away is the hours lost to tasks that were never the best use of your expertise in the first place.

Think About It This Way

A surgeon doesn't resist using the best surgical tools because they fear the tools will replace them. The tools make them more precise, more capable, and more effective. AI is a tool. The best workers will learn to use it with the same level of mastery.

High-Skilled Workers Have the Most to Gain

Here's the counterintuitive truth: AI has the most to offer the people who are already good at what they do.

If you bring deep expertise, strong judgment, and hard-won knowledge to your work, AI gives you leverage you never had before. You can produce more. You can take on more. You can solve problems faster, communicate more clearly, and build systems that scale in ways that previously required a much larger team.

The people most at risk from AI are not the highly skilled. They are the people doing work that never required much judgment to begin with: tasks that are rule-based, repetitive, and don't benefit from context or experience.

Skilled workers using AI don't become redundant. They become rare. The combination of human expertise and AI capability is genuinely hard to replicate. It takes time to develop, requires real understanding of both the domain and the tools, and produces work that neither a human alone nor an AI alone could match.

The Challenge: You Have to Actually Commit to the Shift

This is where I want to be honest, because the opportunity is real but it doesn't come free.

Using AI well is a skill. It is not intuitive, it is not instant, and early attempts often produce mediocre results that confirm people's suspicion that AI "isn't that useful." Getting from there to genuinely powerful AI-augmented work requires deliberate practice, curiosity, and a willingness to feel incompetent while you figure it out.

You have to learn how to prompt well, which is really about learning how to think clearly and communicate precisely. You have to learn which tools fit which tasks. You have to build new habits and workflows. You have to get comfortable iterating and refining rather than expecting perfection on the first attempt.

None of that is unachievable. But it does require making a real decision to invest in it rather than waiting to see what happens.

"The window to build this skill while it's still a differentiator is not infinite. The workers who move now will be years ahead of those who wait for it to become mandatory."

Where to Start

If you're a professional who takes your work seriously and wants to understand what AI augmentation actually looks like in practice, here's my honest advice:

  • Start with your most time-consuming, lowest-judgment tasks. What do you do every week that takes significant time but doesn't require your best thinking? That's where AI can give you the most immediate return.
  • Treat AI as a first-draft partner, not a finished-product machine. The output you'll get from AI is raw material. Your expertise is what turns it into something worth using. Stop expecting AI to replace your judgment and start using it to reduce the time before your judgment gets applied.
  • Get specific about what "better" looks like for your work. Vague goals produce vague results. If you want AI to help you communicate more clearly, write more proposals, or build better systems, know what that looks like so you can tell when it's working.
  • Find people who are doing this well and pay attention to how they work. The fastest path to AI-augmented competence is watching someone who has already built those habits and understanding the thinking behind the tools they use.

This Is a Challenge, Not a Comfort

I'm not writing this to reassure anyone that everything will be fine and AI is nothing to worry about. I'm writing it because I think the reassurance is actually the problem: it keeps people comfortable in a moment that calls for urgency.

The future belongs to people who can do what humans do best and use AI to do everything else faster and better. That is an achievable future. But it requires showing up differently than most people are willing to.

The gap between AI-augmented professionals and everyone else is already growing. The question isn't whether this shift is happening. It's whether you're going to be on the right side of it.

This is the challenge I'm putting in front of every skilled worker I know: don't wait for permission, don't wait for necessity, and don't wait until the window closes. Start now. Build the skill. Refocus your energy on the work that actually requires you, and let AI handle the rest.

That's not a threat to what you do. It's the best possible version of it.
